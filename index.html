Rates Below 6%, Then Above 6.6%: A Volatile First Quarter

In the first three months of 2026, the 30-year fixed mortgage rate swung more than half a percentage point — driven by a presidential directive, a geopolitical standoff, and the outbreak of war in the Middle East.

Data: Mortgage News Daily daily rate index  ·  Freddie Mac Primary Mortgage Market Survey

Mortgage rates have a reputation for moving slowly — a few hundredths of a percent on a typical day, a tenth of a percent on an eventful one. The first quarter of 2026 shattered that pattern.

In January alone, the 30-year fixed rate fell 22 basis points in a single session, then jumped 14 basis points roughly ten days later. By late February, rates had dipped below 6% for the first time since 2022. Then war broke out, and in fewer than four weeks rates climbed back to 6.62%.

Scroll through each market-moving event to see how it unfolded.

January 9, 2026 5.99%

A presidential directive drops rates 22 basis points in a day

On Thursday, January 8, President Trump posted on Truth Social that he was directing Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac to purchase $200 billion in mortgage-backed securities. FHFA Director Bill Pulte confirmed the order the same day, writing "On it" on social media.

5.99% Mortgage News Daily, Jan. 9, 2026  ·  −22 bps from the prior day

By Friday morning, the Mortgage News Daily rate index had fallen from 6.21% to 5.99% — a 22-basis-point drop in a single session, and the first sub-6% reading since February 2023. The move was driven by anticipation: markets repriced the bonds before a single purchase had been executed. FHFA Director Pulte confirmed that $3 billion in purchases had already begun by Friday afternoon.

January 20, 2026 6.21%

The Greenland tariff threat reverses the gains overnight

Over the weekend before Inauguration Day, President Trump threatened 10% tariffs on eight European nations — targeting Germany, France, Denmark, the U.K., and others — to pressure Denmark to cede Greenland. European leaders immediately pushed back, and a Danish pension fund announced it would sell its U.S. Treasury holdings.

6.21% Mortgage News Daily, Jan. 20, 2026  ·  +14 bps from the prior session

The 10-year Treasury yield jumped to 4.3% as investors repriced inflation risk. Mortgage rates closely track the 10-year yield: by Tuesday morning, the Mortgage News Daily index had climbed 14 basis points to 6.21%, exactly reversing the January 9 gain. Trump stepped back from the tariff threat within days — but rates never returned to the 5.99% level reached after the MBS announcement.

February 26, 2026 5.99%

A 3.5-year milestone: below 6% and holding

After the Greenland tariff threat faded, rates drifted steadily lower through February. Anxiety about a proposed new global tariff — paradoxically — drove investors into U.S. Treasuries, pushing yields down. Mortgage rates follow yields.

5.98% Freddie Mac PMMS, week of Feb. 26  ·  First sub-6% weekly average since mid-2022

The Mortgage News Daily index held at 5.99% through the final week of February. On February 26, Freddie Mac's weekly survey recorded 5.98% — the first reading below 6% in three and a half years. Refinance applications surged. The spring homebuying season looked poised for its best start since 2022.

Two days later, everything changed.

March 2, 2026 6.12%

War breaks out — and rates move the wrong way

On Saturday, February 28, the United States and Israel launched joint military strikes against Iran. When bond markets opened Monday morning, they behaved in an unexpected way: yields rose rather than fell.

6.12% Mortgage News Daily, Mar. 2, 2026  ·  +13 bps from the prior week's 5.99% low

Normally, geopolitical shock sends investors into Treasuries — a "flight to safety" that pushes yields lower. This time was different. Iran threatened to blockade the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of the world's oil flows. Oil prices surged past $100 a barrel. Higher energy prices mean higher inflation, and when investors expect sustained inflation, they demand more compensation from long-term bonds — pushing yields, and mortgage rates, higher.

The sub-6% window that had opened over two months of careful market progress closed in 72 hours.

March 18, 2026 6.48%

The Fed holds — and dashes hopes for rate cuts this year

The Federal Reserve voted unanimously at its March meeting to hold its benchmark rate at 3.50–3.75%, citing "uncertain impacts from the war with Iran." But it was the updated projections, not the hold itself, that rattled markets.

6.48% Mortgage News Daily, Mar. 18, 2026  ·  Up +49 bps from the Feb. low in 19 days

Officials raised their core inflation forecast for 2026 to 2.7% and signaled that only one rate cut — at most — remained possible for the year. In January, markets had priced in two or three cuts. That reversal pushed bond yields higher still. The probability of a rate hike, once near zero, climbed above 50% in futures markets for the first time.

March 26, 2026 6.62%

The biggest weekly surge since the original Liberation Day tariffs

By the week of March 24, the Mortgage News Daily index reached 6.62% — the highest level since the summer of 2025 and a climb of 63 basis points from the February 27 low in fewer than four weeks.

6.62% Mortgage News Daily, ~Mar. 26, 2026  ·  +63 bps from the Feb. 27 low of 5.99%

Freddie Mac's weekly survey for the same period recorded 6.38% — its fourth consecutive weekly increase and the largest single-week jump since April 2025, when President Trump's original "Liberation Day" tariff announcement roiled global bond markets. Mortgage applications for home purchases fell for four straight weeks.

On a $400,000 home with 20% down, the difference between the February low and the March peak translated to roughly $150 more per month — about $1,800 per year. For buyers already stretched by high home prices and elevated grocery and gas costs, the reversal arrived at the worst possible moment: the start of the spring selling season.

As of early April 2026, the 30-year fixed rate remains above 6.5% by most daily measures. Economists who expected 2026 to be a recovery year for the housing market are revising their forecasts, with many now projecting another year of depressed transaction volumes.

The first quarter of 2026 illustrated a recurring reality of the post-pandemic mortgage market: rates respond not just to Federal Reserve decisions, but to Truth Social posts, trade threats, oil shocks, and military conflict. For borrowers trying to time their purchase, that unpredictability is the environment they must plan around.
